The idea, in one line: Zeb trains Aaron. Aaron builds Zeb's online business — marketing, web, and video. An even trade between two professionals, each giving the thing they're best at, with clear deliverables on both sides so the handshake stays friendly.

What Each Side Delivers

Zeb → Aaron: Training

  • 2 sessions / week — 30-minute personal training at the studio (schedule flexes around both calendars)
  • Program design — a written plan Aaron follows on non-session days
  • Quarterly assessment — measurements, mobility check, goals reset
  • Nutrition guidance — the same coaching paying clients get

Aaron → Zeb: Marketing / Web / Video

  • Web — build & maintain the ZFIT ecommerce site and landing pages; Stripe checkout setup; updates as products launch
  • Video — film & edit the 10-Minute Series (Abs, Booty, video #3) plus promo cuts for social
  • Marketing — email sequences, social templates, launch campaigns, and the funnel (see below)
  • Reporting — a simple monthly snapshot: list growth, sales, traffic

Ground Rules (The Part That Keeps It Friendly)

Ownership & Money

  • Zeb owns the brand — ZFIT, Zeb's Platinum Fitness, all video content featuring Zeb, and 100% of product revenue
  • Aaron owns his craft — may show the work in his portfolio; reusable templates/code remain Aaron's to reuse elsewhere
  • Hard costs are Zeb's — domain, hosting, Stripe fees, email platform, ad spend (est. $30–60/mo before ads)
  • Optional upside — if ZFIT digital revenue passes $1,000/mo for 3 straight months, discuss a small rev-share (e.g. 10%) to replace traded hours

Boundaries & Exit

  • Scope is written — new asks (e.g. "can you also run my Facebook ads daily") get added to the ledger, not assumed
  • Either side can pause — 2 weeks notice, no hard feelings; ledger balance settles in sessions or work
  • Wind-down — if it ends, Zeb keeps the site & content; Aaron does a 1-hour handoff so anyone can maintain it
  • Not a partnership entity — this is a barter of services between two sole proprietors; each handles their own taxes (barter is reportable income — both should note fair-market value)
